The first time I sent a Chinese factory my product CAD files, I attached a US-style NDA pulled off a template site. The factory signed it the same day. Six months later, a Shenzhen competitor was selling a near-identical product at 30% below my retail.

The NDA was unenforceable in China. It always was. What I needed — and what most importers don't know exists — was an **NNN agreement**.

## NDA vs NNN: the actual difference

A US-style NDA (Non-Disclosure Agreement) restricts the receiving party from disclosing your confidential information. That's it. In a Chinese court, the standard NDA fails on three counts:

1. **Jurisdiction**: it names a US court. Chinese courts will not enforce a foreign judgment in IP cases.
2. **Language**: an English-only contract is at best half-enforceable and at worst thrown out as ambiguous.
3. **Scope**: NDAs don't prohibit the factory from *using* your information themselves or selling a competing product they make. Only from telling others.

An **NNN agreement** (Non-disclosure, Non-use, Non-circumvention) is written specifically for the China context. It adds two clauses an NDA lacks:

- **Non-use**: the factory cannot use your designs, specs, or trade secrets for any purpose other than fulfilling your order. They cannot make the same product for themselves, a competitor, or a different SKU under a different brand.
- **Non-circumvention**: the factory cannot bypass you to reach your customers, suppliers, or business partners.

It's also written in Chinese (the controlling language), names a Chinese court in the supplier's province as the jurisdiction, and specifies **liquidated damages in RMB** — a fixed cash penalty per breach that doesn't require you to prove damages.

## What makes an NNN actually enforceable

Five technical requirements. Miss any of them and the document is decoration.

### 1. Chinese language version is the controlling text

Bilingual is fine — many NNNs have English + Chinese side-by-side. But the agreement must state that **the Chinese version controls** in case of conflict. Chinese courts will read the Chinese.

### 2. Signed by the supplier's legal representative + company chop

The single most important detail. In China, a contract is binding when stamped with the company's official **chop** (red round seal) — not just signed. A signature without a chop is weak; a chop without the right company name is forgery.

Verify the chop matches the supplier's registered company name on their business license. If your sales contact says "the boss is traveling, I'll sign for him" — that signature is worthless.

### 3. Jurisdiction in a Chinese court the supplier's province

You want the dispute heard in the province where the supplier is registered (typically Guangdong, Zhejiang, Jiangsu, or Fujian). Local courts can seize local assets. Specifying "Hong Kong arbitration" or "Stockholm" sounds neutral and is functionally useless — by the time you win, the factory has emptied accounts.

### 4. Liquidated damages, not "actual damages"

Western contracts say "the breaching party will pay damages." Chinese courts hate calculating actual damages and routinely award nothing if you can't prove every dollar.

A good NNN specifies **a fixed penalty per breach** — e.g. RMB 300,000 (≈ $42,000) per unauthorized sale. The court enforces the number on the page. You don't need to prove your lost profits.

Typical liquidated damages clauses I've seen in real NNN agreements range from **RMB 100,000 to RMB 1,000,000** depending on the product value and the supplier's size.

### 5. Survives the contract end

The NNN obligations must extend **at least 3-5 years past** the end of your business relationship. Many template NNNs are silent on this. Without it, the factory is free to clone your product the day after your last order.

## When you actually need one

Not every order needs an NNN. The trade-off is real: a properly drafted bilingual NNN from a China-qualified lawyer costs **$400-1,500**. Suppliers may push back, lose interest, or charge a premium.

You need an NNN when:

- You're sending **custom CAD files, technical drawings, or molds** the supplier didn't already have
- The product has a **defensible design** (utility, not "shaped like a circle")
- You expect order volumes large enough that a knockoff would hurt: $50k+ annual purchase value
- You're planning to **register a patent or design** but haven't yet (the NNN protects the window)

You probably don't need an NNN when:

- You're buying a generic product from a 1688 catalog and putting your logo on it
- Sample-stage orders below $1,000
- The "IP" is just a brand name (use a trademark instead)
- You're dropshipping a product 500 other sellers also offer

## Where to get one drafted

Three options, in order of cost:

**Template + local lawyer review** ($300-500): you start with a Harris Bricken or similar published template, send it to a China-qualified lawyer for review and customization. Good for orders under $50k value.

**Full custom drafting** ($800-1,500): a China-IP lawyer drafts from scratch based on your specific product and risk. Worth it for proprietary products and orders over $50k/year.

**Avoid free template sites** for anything beyond first-look reference. Most online "China NNN templates" are old, missing the chop clause, or written in clumsy translated Chinese that a court will discount.

Lawyers I've seen recommended (not personal endorsements): Harris Sliwoski (Harris Bricken), CLA Legal, or any Beijing/Shanghai-based firm with bilingual IP attorneys. Expect to pay USD-equivalent rates.

## What the supplier will do when you send it

Three reactions, in order of frequency:

1. **Sign without much pushback** (~60% of factories) — especially if they want the order and you're polite about it. Don't apologize for asking; treat it as standard. "This is our standard agreement for all our manufacturers" lands fine.
2. **Negotiate the penalty number down** (~25%) — fine, the existence of any liquidated damages clause is the protection. Don't die on the exact RMB number.
3. **Refuse outright** (~15%) — this is a useful signal. A factory that won't sign basic IP protection on a custom product is telling you something. Walk.

In my experience, mid-size legitimate factories (50-300 employees, established Alibaba presence, real exports) sign NNNs routinely. Tiny workshops and shady trading companies don't.

## The realistic enforcement picture

Even with a perfect NNN, enforcement is not automatic. You still need to:

- Detect the breach (monitoring Alibaba, Amazon, and Chinese marketplaces for clones)
- Hire a Chinese lawyer to file suit in the supplier's local court
- Pay court fees (typically 1-3% of damages claimed, refunded if you win)
- Wait 6-18 months for a verdict
- Pursue enforcement against the supplier's bank accounts or assets

Plenty of importers with valid NNNs never bother enforcing because the math doesn't work for a $30k loss. The NNN's real value is **deterrence** — a factory that signed a binding RMB 500,000 penalty clause thinks twice before cloning your product, because they don't know in advance whether you'll actually sue.
