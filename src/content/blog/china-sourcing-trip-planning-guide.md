A well-planned China sourcing trip can transform your product business. A poorly planned one wastes time and money. This guide gives you a realistic, actionable framework for planning your first trip.

---

## Is a Sourcing Trip Worth It?

First, an honest assessment. A China sourcing trip involves significant cost (flights, hotels, visa, ground transport) and time (typically 7–14 days minimum to make it worthwhile). It makes sense when:

- You're ready to scale beyond test orders and want direct supplier relationships
- You need to evaluate factories in person before committing to significant production
- You want access to markets like Yiwu or Guangzhou that aren't fully replicable online
- You're developing custom or private label products that require in-person sampling and approval

If you're still testing your market and haven't validated product-market fit, focusing on online sourcing first is more efficient. Come in person when you have clarity on what you're building.

## Step 1: Get Your Visa

Most nationalities need a Chinese visa to enter mainland China. Apply well in advance — don't leave this to the last minute.

### Tourist Visa (L Visa) — Most Common for Sourcing Trips

A standard L visa (tourist) works for sourcing market visits. You don't need a business visa for attending wholesale markets or the Canton Fair.

**Application process:**
1. Find your nearest Chinese embassy or consulate
2. Complete the visa application form online or in person
3. Submit: application form, passport, passport photo, itinerary, hotel bookings
4. Pay visa fee
5. Processing time: 4–7 business days for standard; 2–3 days for express (additional fee)

**Validity options:** Single entry, double entry, or multiple entry (multiple entry requires justification/previous China travel history)

### Visa-Free Entry (Check Current Policy)

China has significantly expanded visa-free entry for many countries in 2024–2025. Citizens of certain countries can enter visa-free for 15–30 days depending on the agreement. Check the current policy for your passport — this changes and is worth verifying.

## Step 2: Plan Your Cities and Timeline

The three most productive cities for most sourcing buyers:

| City | Best For | Days Recommended |
|---|---|---|
| **Guangzhou** | Fashion, garments, fabrics, Canton Fair, factory visits | 3–4 days |
| **Yiwu** | Small commodities, gifts, accessories, testing new products | 2–3 days |
| **Shenzhen** | Electronics, tech accessories, factories, Hong Kong border | 2–3 days |

### Sample 10-Day Itinerary

**Days 1–3: Guangzhou**
- Day 1: Arrive, check in, recover from travel, orientation walk near hotel
- Day 2: Shahe clothing markets + Yide Road toys/gifts
- Day 3: Zhongda fabric market + any factory visits arranged in nearby Dongguan or Foshan

**Days 4–6: Yiwu**
- Take high-speed train from Guangzhou (3.5 hours or fly)
- Day 4: Districts 1 and 2 (jewelry, accessories, toys)
- Day 5: Districts 3 and 4 (hardware, cosmetics, bags)
- Day 6: District 5 + follow-up visits to shortlisted vendors from days 4–5

**Days 7–9: Shenzhen**
- Take high-speed train from Yiwu via Hangzhou/Nanjing (4–5 hours) or fly
- Day 7: Huaqiangbei electronics market
- Day 8: Factory visits in surrounding areas (arrange in advance)
- Day 9: Buffer day — follow-up meetings, additional purchases, or side trip to Hong Kong

**Day 10: Departure**

### If You're Attending Canton Fair

The Canton Fair runs twice yearly (spring and autumn) in Guangzhou. Structure your trip so Canton Fair attendance is days 2–4, then travel to Yiwu and Shenzhen. This makes Guangzhou your natural starting point.

## Step 3: Pre-Trip Preparation

### Supplier Hit List

Before you leave, build a list of:
- Suppliers you've already communicated with online that you want to meet in person
- Products you want to evaluate that you haven't been able to assess from photos
- Factories to visit (arranged with the supplier in advance)

Arriving without a plan is how buyers wander for days without accomplishing much.

### Sample Brief Document

Prepare a document for each product you're sourcing:
- Product specification (dimensions, materials, colors)
- Target price range
- Target quantity for initial order
- Reference photos
- Deal-breaker quality issues

Having this on your phone means you can quickly show any vendor exactly what you need.

### Set Up Your Phone for China

- **Google Maps** works in China but needs a VPN for full functionality. Download offline maps before departure.
- **Install WeChat** — Essential for communicating with vendors during and after the trip
- **Install DiDi** — China's Uber equivalent. Much more convenient than hailing taxis.
- **Alipay or WeChat Pay** — Set up for small payments (meals, transport)
- **VPN** — Install before entering China; VPN app stores may not be accessible from within China. ExpressVPN and NordVPN are commonly used.
- **Google Translate** — Download Chinese language pack for offline use

### Cash and Cards

- Withdraw Chinese Yuan (RMB) at airport ATMs on arrival
- Bring USD $200–$300 as backup (can be exchanged at banks)
- Major hotels accept international credit cards
- Markets and small vendors prefer WeChat Pay/Alipay or cash

## Step 4: What to Bring

**Business cards** — At least 200. Card exchange is essential in Chinese business culture.

**Portable power bank** — Your phone is your translator, map, and camera. Keep it charged.

**Lightweight bags** — For carrying samples and catalogs. Markets give you bags but they're often flimsy.

**Comfortable walking shoes** — You will walk 15,000–25,000 steps per day. This is non-negotiable.

**Notebook or notepad** — Some buyers prefer analog notes; phone battery dies at the worst times.

**Small ruler or flexible tape measure** — For checking product dimensions.

**Laptop** — For reviewing catalog files, photos, and doing order calculations in the evenings.

---

## Step 5: At the Markets — How to Work Effectively

**Morning: Attack your priority targets first.** Your energy is highest in the morning. Use it on the most important vendors.

**Take photos of everything.** Booth number, business card, product, price discussion on calculator. You will not remember which vendor had what by the end of the day.

**Get WeChat contacts, not just business cards.** Cards get lost; WeChat is permanent.

**Don't buy in bulk at the market.** The markets are for evaluation, not final purchasing. Collect information, evaluate quality, get pricing — then follow up with formal orders after you return.

**Keep a simple spreadsheet.** Product name, vendor name, booth number, quoted price, MOQ, notes. Review each evening.

**Walk the whole section before deep conversations.** Don't commit to long conversations with the first vendor before knowing what's available two booths down.

---

## Step 6: After the Trip — Follow-Up

The trip generates leads. The work of converting them into orders happens afterward.

Within 2 weeks of returning:
1. **Shortlist vendors** based on notes and photos
2. **Send detailed RFQs** to your top candidates
3. **Request samples** for your final shortlist
4. **Continue communication via WeChat** — this is now your primary channel

A sourcing trip without strong follow-up produces little return on investment.

## Budget Estimate

For a 10-day China sourcing trip from US/UK/Australia:

| Item | Estimated Cost |
|---|---|
| Flights (economy, roundtrip) | $700–$1,500 |
| Accommodation (10 nights, mid-range) | $600–$1,200 |
| Local transport (metro, DiDi, trains between cities) | $150–$300 |
| Meals | $150–$300 |
| Visa fee | $140–$200 |
| Sample purchases | $200–$500 |
| Miscellaneous | $100–$200 |
| **Total** | **$2,000–$4,200** |

This investment makes most sense when you have clarity on your products and realistic order volumes that will yield returns of many times the trip cost.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is China safe for solo foreign travelers?
Yes. China is generally very safe for travelers, including solo foreign visitors. Standard urban common sense applies — watch your belongings in crowded markets, be cautious with unsolicited assistance.

### Do I need to speak Chinese?
Not fluently, but some basics help significantly. Translation apps handle most situations. Having even 10–15 business phrases (greetings, "how much," "too expensive") builds goodwill. In major sourcing cities, you'll encounter vendors with basic English regularly.

### Should I hire a local guide?
For your first trip, a half-day with a local sourcing guide in each major market is money well spent. They know layouts, shortcut to relevant sections, and can negotiate in Chinese. Expect to pay ¥300–¥800 per day for a professional market guide.

### What's the best time of year to go?
Avoid Chinese New Year (late January/February) — factories and markets largely shut for 2–3 weeks. Autumn (September–October) is peak season for placing orders for holiday merchandise. Spring (April–May) works well for most categories and coincides with the Canton Fair spring session.
